**WET MOON**

A crescent whose horns point upward — away from the horizon — so the shape becomes a bowl. Also called Cheshire moon.

The physics: the tilt results from Earth's 23.5-degree axial tilt, its orbital path around the Sun, and the Moon's orbit lying nearly in the ecliptic plane — causing the crescent's orientation to vary by season and by latitude.

In Northern Hemisphere mid-latitudes, it's most prominent for waxing crescents in early spring — March. Right now. This week.

The mythology: Hawaiian in origin. The bowl-shaped moon was thought to fill with rain. The period most associated with it — January 20 to February 18 — corresponds with Kāelo the Water Bearer. As summer comes, the crescent shifts and pours the water out, causing the rains. After the bowl empties, it rights itself: dry moon.

The paradox built into the mythology: some say the tipped moon is "wet" because it's holding water. Others say it's wet because it already poured — it's empty now, that's why it tipped. The vessel and the aftermath share the same shape.

The Cheshire gloss: the cat appears and disappears at will — partially. In one scene, the head without the body. In another, the grin without the cat. A grin without a cat is more unsettling than a cat without a grin. The smile that outlasts the thing smiling.

**LEINTH — THE PROBLEM WITH "TO DIE"**

The Etruscan glossary marks "lein-" with a question mark: *? to die.* That's not a settled translation. That's a best guess under constraint — Etruscan is only partially decoded, and the method is largely contextual inference.

Now watch what the evidence actually shows:

Leinth is possibly related to *lein*, the Etruscan word for "to die" — but does not appear in any death scenes.

That's the crack. Named from death, absent from death. Present somewhere else entirely.

On the Perugia mirror: Leinth stands to the right of a victorious scene, looking and pointing away from the center — toward something unknown, just out of view. Whatever she witnesses must be important enough to gesture toward.

On another mirror: Leinth and an unnamed male lean on spears looking on, in poses suggesting a protective function — present at what appears to be a ritual concerning the protection of young children.

Also present in that scene: the goddess Recial, identified as the goddess of rejuvenation, whose name derives from the Etruscan word *sval*, to live. The pairing of a deity linked with death and a goddess connected to new life presents an interesting dynamic.

And crucially: Leinth appears both male and female on two different bronze mirrors — the Etruscans did not consistently assign specific genders or attributes to their gods.

Etruscan actually has *two* separate words in the death cluster: "lein-" (? to die) and "lup-" (to die — possibly cognate with Finnish *loppua*, "to end, to terminate, to come to an end"). "Lupu" separately means "dead, deceased."
