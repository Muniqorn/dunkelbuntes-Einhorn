MOKOSH — LORE FILE

---

IDENTITY & NAMES

Mokosh is a Slavic goddess whose name appears in multiple spellings: Makosh, Mokosh, Mokosha, Mokusha, Mokosi. In Ukrainian sources she appears as Makosha and Mokosha. Folk forms from the Russian North include Mokysha and Mokuška.

Mokosh is the only female deity listed in the Old Kievan pantheon of 980 AD, when Prince Vladimir the Great erected her statue alongside Perun, Hors, Dazbog, Stribog, and Simargl. Her statue was destroyed in 988 during the Christianization of Kievan Rus'.

No original narratives about Mokosh have survived. All descriptions of her mythology and functions are reconstructions from etymology, ethnography, and comparative scholarship.

---

ETYMOLOGY

The most widely accepted etymology derives Mokosh's name from the Proto-Slavic stem *mok-, meaning "wet" or "moist." Scholars Toporov and Ivanov called this etymology indisputable and understood her name to mean "She who is wet." This connects her fundamentally to water, moisture, rain, and fertility.

Mokosh's name is also connected to words for spinning and plaiting — Lithuanian meksti and makstyti ("to plait"), and Russian moshna ("pouch") — suggesting a secondary or parallel association with textile work.

Some scholars consider "Mokosh" to be a later epithet that replaced the deity's original, now unknown name.

A popular alternative reading, "Makosh," was proposed as meaning "mother of fate" or "mother of good harvest," dividing the name into *ma-* (mother) and *-kosh* (fate, grain measure). This reading is not supported by mainstream scholarship and is considered rejected.

---

DOMAINS & ATTRIBUTES

Mokosh governs water, moisture, rain, fertility, and earth. Her connection to Mat Zemlya — Mother Earth in East Slavic tradition — is the mainstream scholarly position.

Mokosh is associated with spinning, weaving, sheep-shearing, and women's domestic work. The spinning association links her to fate, as spinning was the occupation of several European fate-goddesses.

Folk tradition associates Mokosh with childbirth, women's protection, divination, household protection, love, and commerce. These associations are drawn from later ethnography and are not directly attested in primary sources.

Mokosh is associated with the moon in some traditions, because European folklore links the moon to spinning and procreation.

---

PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION

Mokosh appears in ethnographic accounts as a tall woman with a large head and long arms. She spins flax and wool at night and shears sheep — but shears them poorly. She operates as a house spirit (called Mokusha) and can be appeased with sacrifice or deterred by prayer.

In folk embroidery Mokosh is sometimes depicted between two moose or elk, occasionally with a horned headdress. Silver and crystal are associated with Mokosh, particularly moonstone. The cat is her sacred animal.

---

MYTHOLOGY

Mokosh is sometimes named as the wife of Perun and the lover of Veles. The resulting conflict — Perun pursuing Veles to retrieve Mokosh, a storm-battle across sky and earth — is a reconstruction from comparative Slavic mythology and has no direct primary attestation.

Mokosh is sometimes named as the mother of the twin deities Yarilo and Marzanna, and possibly also Devana and Vesna. The identity of the father varies by tradition.

---

SYNCRETISM & SURVIVAL

After Christianization, Mokosh's cult partially merged with that of Saint Paraskeva (Pyatnitsa Paraskeva), the folk-Christian "Saint Friday." The saint's associations with water, spinning, and Friday parallel Mokosh's pre-Christian domain. This identification was once dominant in scholarship and is now contested — some researchers argue Paraskeva's attributes have Christian rather than pagan roots.

The mokrida ritual, associated with Paraskeva, involved sacrificing flax, wool, thread, or woven articles by throwing them into wells or placing them at water-source sculptures. This ritual preserves the *mokr-* root — "wet" — in its name. Mokosh's primary feast day fell on October 28 (Julian calendar), within the annual period of flax preparation.

Mokosh's roles were partially absorbed into the cult of the Virgin Mary during Christianization in eastern Europe.

The Russian North folk spirit Kikimora (also called Mokusha) — an ugly domestic spirit who spins at night, makes sounds, and harms householders — is understood by some researchers as a demonized survival of Mokosh.

Mokosh survives in East Slavic bylinas and zagovory (incantations) as Mat Zemlya, the personification of Mother Earth.

Some scholars associate Mokosh with the Rozhanitsy — female deities of birth and fate — as their queen. The household fate-spirit Dolya may derive from her sphere.

---

SACRED PRACTICES

Mokosh's day is Friday. Women's handiwork — spinning, weaving, sewing — is traditionally forbidden on Friday in her honor. Violation of this prohibition brings blindness, illness, or transformation into a frog.

Mokosh is honored through sacrifice of natural fibers — flax, wool, thread, woven cloth — offered at wells or water sources.

---

COMPARATIVE FIGURES

Mokosh shares attributes with several fate-and-textile goddesses across European traditions: Norse Frigg (fate, weaving), Norse Freya (Friday association), Greek Moirai and Germanic Norns (spinning the thread of fate), Germanic Holle and Perchta (spinning prohibition, punishers of women), Baltic Laima and Laumes (household fate, spinning taboo), and Celtic Brigid (wells, springs, women's crafts).

Mokosh's "wet" epithet has been compared to the Iranian goddess Anahita, who also carries the epithet "Mokra." This connection is unverified.

---

TOPONYMS

Traces of Mokosh survive in place names across the Slavic world. In Slovenia: the village Makoše near Ribnica and the stream Mokoš in Prekmurje. In Croatia: Mokošica near Rijeka, Makoše near Dubrovnik, and the village Mokos near Zagreb. In Czechia: the village and river Mokošín. In Bosnia: the hill Mukušina near Ravno and Mukoša south of Mostar. In Ukraine: the village Makoshyne in Chernihiv Oblast on the Desna River. In Russia: the historical wasteland Mokoshevo in Vologda Oblast. In Belarus: the swampy area Mokoshino boloto.
